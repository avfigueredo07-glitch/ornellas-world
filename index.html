The Woman Behind the Words

A doctor, a mama,
and a believer in stories

Ornella didn't set out to become a children's author — she became one the moment she searched every bookshelf for a story that reflected her children's beautiful, blended world and couldn't find it. So she wrote it herself.

As a doctor and a mother, Ornella understands that children are whole people: emotionally complex, culturally rich, and hungry for validation. Her books are prescriptions of a different kind — stories that say "your food, your language, your roots — these are things to be proud of."

"I write the books I needed when my children were small. Not just good stories — stories that feel like home."

Born with Italian heritage and raising her family across cultures, Ornella weaves her lived experience as an immigrant mother into every page. Her debut book, Pasta, Arepa and Hamburger, was born from her own kitchen table — a place where cultures collide deliciously and every meal tells a story.

— Ornella

⭐ Featured Book

Pasta, Arepa and Hamburger

By Dr. Ornella Lira Spinali De Figueredo

As a mom who grew up between two cultures, I know exactly what it feels like to want your children to see themselves in a book — and this one delivers that in the most joyful, delicious way. Pasta, Arepa and Hamburger is the story of a family just like so many of ours: multilingual, multicultural, and connected by a shared table. Ornella captures the magic of a kitchen where grandmother's pasta sits beside Mom's arepas and Dad's hamburgers, showing children that different is beautiful, not confusing. I read it to my little one three nights in a row — requested every time. This is the kind of book that lives on the nightstand, not the shelf.

Why I Write Books About Food (And What I'm Really Saying)

People always ask me why my first children's book is about pasta, arepas, and hamburgers. "Why food?" they say. And I always smile because the real answer is: because food is never just food. It's memory. It's identity. It's your grandmother's hands and the country you left behind and the new life you're building.

When I sit down to write a children's book, I write from the part of me that is a mother first. I think about the moment my child opens a page and either sees themselves — or doesn't. I want every child of mixed heritage, every child of immigrant parents, every child raised at a table with two languages, to open one of my books and exhale.
